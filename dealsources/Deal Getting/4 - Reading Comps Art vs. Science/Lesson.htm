A Word from JP Moses, Director of Awesome...

One of the most important aspects of being a real estate investor is knowing how to assess a deal -- how to analyze whether or not it's a good deal or a bad deal. Add of course, one of the key components of this process is reading comparables (comps) to determine the current values in a specific market.

But this isn't always as easy and intuitive as it sounds, is it? In fact, there are some rather noteworthy mistakes we see a lot of investors making when reading comps -- mistakes that ultimately lead to poor evaluation, which leads of course to stinky deals.

The following is Mogul faculty Steve Cook's take on some of the biggest mistakes he sees investors making when reading comps, and more importantly, how to avoid them.

       Pull out a current deal file: One you're working on now or a recent one, along with the comps (if you don't have one right now, pull up another investor friend's file)

       Ask yourself: Are you taking the larger market into consideration?

       Broaden your perspective: Dont focus only on exact match homes exclusivelythis can lead to tunnel vision and only gives you part of the picture.

       Start: taking a more holistic view of the neighborhood youre deal is in not focusing only on one particular property/type.

       Remember: to comp using properties that are comparable to what your home will become rather than what it is now.

Hi, Alan. If all comps are foreclosures, then these foreclosures are inevitably going to effect your property's resale, even if you sell it retail. Keep that in mind. 

If you're really not able to get any "normal" comps, then you may need to expand your parameters. Go up to a mile away + 3 months old. Then increase up to 2 miles way if you need to...or go up to 6 months old. See what that brings up. It's a little riskier, but sometimes you gotta do it that way.

As for where the best comps come from, I almost always say MLS trumps everything. Though there may be local options that can compete. For example, here in Memphis we have something called "The Chandler Report" that's as good as the MLS, and you don't have to be a Realtor to get it. But it's not cheap. We also have Courthouse Retrieval Service in TN...it's good for comps...and again, you don't have to be a Realtor. So ask around locally and see if there's a competitive service near you.

Many folks I know use Realquest.com for comps. Alex Joungblood and Cris Chico among them. So that's worth looking into.

Whatever you do, don't depend on free services like Zillow for your comps.

Mike Gagg

As a REALTOR, I eliminate all REO and Short Sales from my comp pool. If your plan is to fix and flip, distressed properties are not comparable. Although the appraisers are changing things a bit and if there is a high volume of distressed properties in the area, they may end up including an REO or SS. I went ballistic on an appraiser that did this to me a few months ago. Gave me a $97K appraisal when I had another one that said $130K. I turned around and sold it for $143K instead of trying to refi and hold it.

As far as doing comps outside of the MLS (before I got my license), I found Trulia.com worked the best for me. There's a "recently sold" tab (or something like that) and you can actually drill down into all solds. Some newer investors may try and take the Zestimate or the Trulia Estimate for a given house. While they have gotten those algorithms working a little better, I can still find large discrepancies in values.
