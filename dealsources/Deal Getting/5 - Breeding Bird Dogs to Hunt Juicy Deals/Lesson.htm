Hi, Terry - I'm not sure I totally get where you're coming from. Steve's lesson that you linked to above is about breeding others to be bird dogs for you. But your question seems to be coming from the perspective of you being a bird dog. Which is perfectly legit...but it makes me think it's possible you might have missed the heart of Steve's lesson, for what it's worth.

If you're concerned about giving up bigger checks by being a bird dog versus a wholesaler...that's the trade off for lower risk. Being a bird dog involves less risk than being a wholesaler... and typically requires much less due diligence. Just like being a wholesaler involves a lot less time and risk than a fix-and-flip retailed...and also lower checks than a rehabber too. With each step up in risk and effort, the checks tend to get bigger. 

Not saying one's better than the other...just explaining why it makes sense. Frankly I strongly prefer wholesaling. The quick turn model suits my personality and style best after more than a dozen years now.

Hi Deb, I am not a "Realtor," but Licensing laws come into play as I'm a Lic. Broker. With that said, I too have struggled with the law, but I think I have the solution. Your offer on Craislist, etc., according to your state's laws, may have to include that you are an agent or broker, so, you will have to abide by that, or should anyway. If you use the first method, and pay the Birddog when it's assigned, the Birddog is an "owner," even for the brief minute when passing papers. Therefore, by assigning it, he is "profiting," from the transaction and it would not be considered a RE commission to an unlicensed agent. The Birddog is a buffer between the original owner and you. This is probably why Steve prefers this method, but you would have to pay the 10K fee at the time of transfer to you. If you are not paying the Birddog at the time of assignment, and you prefer the alternative route, then, as Steve mentions, the Birddog will be considered a partner as in joint venture, and will be paid from the proceeds when the property is flipped. The only other way to deal with the law, that I know of, is to have an executed simple agreement and a signed W9 that states that he/she will "work" for you as a 1099 sub-contractor who finds suitable properties in exchange for a marketing fee. I do like this whole idea, and I will be implementing it myself in the near future.
